**"This is ‚Ç¶45,000? That's too expensive."**

**What just happened?**

1. You trained them that your prices are negotiable (they'll always ask for discounts now)
2. You made the product seem less valuable (if you can discount 10%, was it ever worth full price?)
3. You didn't address the real issue (they don't see why it's worth ‚Ç¶45K)

**Price objections aren't about the number. They're about perceived value.**

## üí∞ WHY THIS COSTS YOU SALES (AND MARGIN)

Let's say you discount 10% on every "it's too expensive" objection:

**Daily discount damage:**
- 3 customers/day ask for discount
- You give 10% off ‚Ç¶30,000 average basket = ‚Ç¶3,000 margin lost/sale
- 3 sales √ó ‚Ç¶3,000 = **‚Ç¶9,000/day** in margin given away
- ‚Ç¶9,000 √ó 6 days = ‚Ç¶54,000/week
- **‚Ç¶2.8M/year in margin** you didn't need to sacrifice

**But it's worse than that:**

**Training effect:**
- Word spreads: "If you ask for discount, they'll give it"
- Now 5 customers/day ask (even when they'd have paid full price)
- ‚Ç¶5M/year in margin evaporated

**Devaluation effect:**
- Customers think: "If they can discount 10%, it was never worth the original price"
- Brand perception drops
- Premium positioning collapses

**The real cost:** You're not just losing money‚Äîyou're destroying your ability to charge what you're worth.

## üß† WHAT "IT'S TOO EXPENSIVE" ACTUALLY MEANS

When a customer says "it's too expensive," they're usually saying one of these:

### Translation #1: "I don't see why it's worth this much"
They don't understand the value difference between this and a cheaper version. Your job: show them.

### Translation #2: "It's more than I planned to spend"
They came in with a budget. You showed them something above it. They like it, but need permission to spend more.

### Translation #3: "I can get something similar for less"
They're comparing to a cheaper brand or knock-off. They don't see the quality difference yet.

### Translation #4: "I want a discount but don't know how to ask"
They assume everything is negotiable. They're testing if you'll budge.

### Translation #5: "I actually can't afford this"
They literally don't have the money. No amount of value justification will change that.

**Your job:** Figure out which one it is‚Äîthen respond accordingly.

## ‚úÖ WHAT TO DO INSTEAD

### Step 1: Don't Panic, Don't Defend, Don't Discount

**Don't say:** "But it's really good quality!" ‚ùå
(Sounds defensive, not convincing)

**Don't say:** "I can give you 10% off." ‚ùå
(Throws away margin before you even try)

**Don't say:** "It's actually not that expensive for [brand]." ‚ùå
(Dismisses their concern, sounds condescending)

**Instead, say:** "I hear you. What were you thinking price-wise?"

**Why this works:**
- Acknowledges their concern (doesn't dismiss it)
- Reveals their actual budget (now you know what you're working with)
- Keeps the conversation going (doesn't end with "okay, never mind")

---

### Step 2: Anchor the Value (Not the Price)

Once you know their budget, re-anchor them to **why this product costs what it costs.**

**Bad value anchoring (talking about the brand):**
"Well, it's PUMA. PUMA is always premium quality."

**Good value anchoring (connecting to their specific need):**

"I totally get it. The reason this one is ‚Ç¶45K instead of ‚Ç¶25K is [specific benefit they cared about]. You mentioned [thing they said earlier]‚Äîthis solves that. The ‚Ç¶25K version doesn't."

**Example:**

Customer: "‚Ç¶45,000 for a hoodie? That's a lot."

You: "What were you thinking price-wise?"

Customer: "Probably ‚Ç¶25-30K."

You: "Okay, so here's the difference. You said you wear hoodies all day for work, right? This one [hold it up] has reinforced elbows and a heavier weight fabric. Most ‚Ç¶25K hoodies start pilling after 3-4 washes. This one lasts 2-3 years. You're paying ‚Ç¶45K once instead of replacing a ‚Ç¶25K hoodie 3 times. It's actually cheaper long-term."

**What just happened:**
1. You reframed price as **cost per wear** (not cost per item)
2. You connected it to **their specific use case** (all-day wear)
3. You compared to the **cheap alternative** (and showed why it fails)
4. You made ‚Ç¶45K sound like a smart investment, not an expensive splurge

---

### Step 3: The "Show Down" Strategy

If they're still hesitant, **show them the cheaper option.**

"Want to see what the ‚Ç¶25K version looks like? I'll show you both side by side."

Then physically compare:
- Hold both items up
- Point out fabric thickness, stitching quality, zipper durability
- Let them feel the difference

**What usually happens:** They realize the ‚Ç¶45K version is worth it.

**If they still choose ‚Ç¶25K:** You saved the sale (‚Ç¶25K > ‚Ç¶0). And you preserved trust (you helped them, didn't push them).

**Why this works:**
- You're not dismissing their budget
- You're empowering them to make an informed decision
- Seeing the downgrade often makes them appreciate the upgrade

---

### Step 4: The Payment Reframe

If they love it but truly can't afford ‚Ç¶45K upfront, **reframe how they're paying.**

"Just so you know, we have installment options‚Äî‚Ç¶15K today, ‚Ç¶15K next month, ‚Ç¶15K the month after. Does that make it easier?"

**Or break it down per use:**

"If you wear this 3 times a week for a year, that's 150 wears. ‚Ç¶45K √∑ 150 = ‚Ç¶300 per wear. That's less than the cost of lunch."

**Why this works:** ‚Ç¶45,000 feels big. ‚Ç¶15,000 √ó 3 or ‚Ç¶300/wear feels manageable.

## üöß COMMON MISTAKES

**Mistake 1: Discounting immediately**
Customer: "It's too expensive."
You: "I can give you 10% off!"

**What you just did:** Trained them to always ask for discounts. And you devalued your product. Never discount before you've tried value anchoring.

---

**Mistake 2: Getting defensive**
Customer: "‚Ç¶35K for a T-shirt? That's crazy."
You: "It's PUMA! Of course it costs that much!"

**What you just did:** Made them feel stupid for questioning the price. They'll leave to save face.

---

**Mistake 3: Comparing to luxury brands**
"This is actually cheap compared to Nike Air Max at ‚Ç¶60K."

**What you just did:** Anchored them to a different tier. They weren't comparing to Nike‚Äîthey were comparing to ‚Ç¶20K options.

**Mistake 4: Insulting cheaper alternatives**
"Those cheap knock-offs fall apart in a week."

**What you just did:** Insulted their budget. Now it's personal. Instead, say: "The lower-priced versions don't have [feature you need], but they're fine if you don't need that."

---

**Mistake 5: Over-explaining the brand**
"PUMA has been making athletic wear since 1948, and the founder was actually the brother of the Adidas founder, and‚Ä¶"

**What you just did:** Bored them. They don't care about brand history. They care about whether this solves their problem.

## üß† WHY THIS WORKS

**Here's the secret: Premium pricing is a sales tool, not a barrier.**

Customers who see high prices assume high quality. That's why luxury brands never discount‚Äîit would destroy the perception.

But here's what most staff don't understand: **"Too expensive" is rarely about the actual money.**

It's about:
- Justification ("Can I justify spending this much?")
- Comparison ("Is this better than the cheaper version?")
- Trust ("Is this actually worth it, or are you upselling me?")

**When you anchor value properly‚Äîconnecting price to their specific need‚Äîyou're not convincing them. You're giving them permission to buy.**

One retail director said it perfectly:
> "If a customer complains about price, they're actually interested. They wouldn't mention price if they'd already decided not to buy. Price objections are buying signals in disguise."

**Your job:** Help them see why the price is fair. Then let them decide.
