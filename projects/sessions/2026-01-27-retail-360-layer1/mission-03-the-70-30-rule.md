## üí∞ WHY THIS COSTS YOU SALES

A Nike manager described this exact issue:

> "They don't listen to the customer to find out what the customer is actually looking for and focus on that. They just give their full spiel, and they don't identify‚Äîokay, this is a customer who's in his 40s, he needs more support."

Here's what happens when you talk more than you listen:

**1. The customer is overwhelmed**
Too many options. Too much information. They can't process it all. Decision paralysis kicks in. "I'll think about it" = "I need to escape."

**2. You miss the real need**
They wanted shoes for their sore back. You showed them 5 options based on style. You never solved their actual problem.

**3. You lose trust**
The customer senses you're not listening. They feel like you're trying to move product, not help them. That kills trust fast.

**4. You can't handle objections later**
When they say "I'll think about it," you don't know what they're thinking about. You never found out what actually mattered to them.

## ‚úÖ WHAT TO DO INSTEAD

### The 70/30 Rule

**In the browsing phase:**
- **Customer should talk 70% of the time**
- **You should talk 30% of the time**

If you're doing most of the talking, you're doing it wrong.

Your job is to:
1. **Ask questions**
2. **Listen to answers**
3. **Ask follow-up questions**
4. **THEN** recommend based on what you learned

Not:
1. Show them everything
2. Talk about features
3. Hope something sticks

---

## üéØ THE 4 QUESTIONS FRAMEWORK

Before you show a single product, understand these four things:

### Question 1: THE OCCASION
"What are you buying for?"

Not "I need shoes" ‚Äî dig deeper. "I'm running my first marathon" or "I need something for work that's comfortable" or "My old ones fell apart."

**Why it matters:** The occasion tells you context‚Äîhow they'll use it, what matters.

**Follow-ups:**
- "Where will you be wearing this?"
- "What activities do you need this for?"
- "What's coming up that made you come in today?"

---

### Question 2: THE PROBLEM
"What's not working with what you have now?"

Every customer is here because something isn't working. Find out what.

**Why it matters:** The problem tells you which features actually matter (and which don't).

**Follow-ups:**
- "What do you wish your current [item] did better?"
- "Have you tried anything before that didn't work?"
- "What's frustrating you about your current setup?"

**Example:**
- You: "What made you want new running shoes?"
- Customer: "My knees have been killing me."
- You: **[Now you know]** ‚Üí Show cushioned shoes, not speed shoes.

---

### Question 3: THE PRIORITY
"What matters most‚Äîcomfort, durability, or style?"

Everyone weighs these differently. Some people don't care what it looks like if it's comfortable. Others need it to look good above everything else.

**Why it matters:** This tells you how to frame your recommendation.

**Follow-ups:**
- "Do you have a budget in mind?"
- "Is brand important to you?"
- "Are you looking for something that'll last years, or just this season?"

---

### Question 4: THE DECISION CONTEXT
"Are you hoping to leave with something today?"

Are they buying today or researching? Buying for themselves or others? Alone or need to consult someone?

**Why it matters:** This tells you how urgently to push, whether to suggest they bring someone back, whether to get their number for follow-up.

**Follow-ups:**
- "Is this for you or someone else?"
- "Is there anyone else involved in the decision?"
- "How soon do you need this?"

## üöß COMMON MISTAKES

**Mistake 1: Asking questions but not listening to answers**
You ask "What kind of running?" but you're already thinking about which shoe to show. Stop. Actually listen.

**Mistake 2: Asking too many questions at once**
One question at a time. Let them answer. Then ask a follow-up based on what they said.

**Mistake 3: Jumping to recommendations too fast**
If you're showing products after 30 seconds of conversation, you probably don't understand what they need yet. Slow down.

**Mistake 4: Feeling like you need to know everything**
It's okay to say "That's a great question‚Äîlet me find out for you." Better than making something up.

## üß† WHY THIS WORKS

**Product knowledge is important‚Äîbut only after you understand the customer's need.**

A customer doesn't care about React foam. They care about their knees not hurting.

A customer doesn't care about thread count. They care about sheets feeling softer than what they have now.

**When you ask questions first, you can connect features to needs.** That's when features matter.

> "If you give me a trained lady who can sell, she can sell anything. But if you give me someone who just knows the product inside out but doesn't know the routine? They'll feature dump and the customer will leave."

**Routine beats knowledge.**
