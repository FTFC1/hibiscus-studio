## üß† WHAT "I'LL THINK ABOUT IT" ACTUALLY MEANS

When a customer says "I'll think about it," they're usually saying one of these things:

### Translation #1: "I'm not sure this is worth the price"
They like it, but they're not convinced the value justifies the cost. They need more anchoring to the benefit, not more features.

### Translation #2: "I need to ask someone"
Spouse, parent, friend. They're not the final decision-maker, but they don't want to admit it. (Especially men buying clothes‚Äîoften need approval but won't say it.)

### Translation #3: "Something's not quite right, but I don't want to be rude"
Could be fit, color, style, quality concern. They'd rather exit politely than voice a complaint.

### Translation #4: "I want to compare prices elsewhere"
They're checking if you're the cheapest option. They won't admit this because it sounds cheap.

### Translation #5: "I'm actually just browsing and you pressured me"
You pushed too hard, too fast. Now they're using "I'll think about it" to escape the conversation.

**Your job:** Figure out which one it is. You can't solve a problem you don't understand.

## ‚úÖ WHAT TO DO INSTEAD

### Step 1: Acknowledge Without Surrendering

**Don't say:** "Okay, let me know if you need anything." ‚ùå
This ends the conversation. You've accepted defeat.

**Don't say:** "But wait‚Äîthis is really great!" ‚ùå
Desperation repels. Pushiness kills trust.

**Instead, say:** "That's totally fair. Can I ask‚Äîwhat's making you want to think it over?"

**Why this works:**
- Acknowledges their boundary ("that's fair")
- Asks permission to probe ("can I ask")
- Open-ended question ("what's making you...")
- Doesn't assume the problem (lets them tell you)

---

### Step 2: Listen to What They Say (and Don't Say)

**If they say:** "I just need to think about it."
**What they mean:** They're not ready to tell you the real reason yet.

**Your move:** Offer a specific guess (gives them permission to agree).

"I totally get it. Is it the price, or is it more about finding the right fit?"

**Why this works:** You've named two common concerns. If it's one of those, they'll often admit it: "Yeah, the price is a bit more than I was expecting." Now you can address it.

---

**If they say:** "I need to check with my wife/husband."
**What they mean:** They're not the decision-maker (and that's okay).

**Your move:** Make it easy for them to come back or include the other person.

"Makes sense! Do you want to take a photo to show them? Or I can hold this for you until tomorrow if you want to come back together."

**Why this works:** You're helping them, not trapping them. They'll appreciate the flexibility‚Äîand they're more likely to return.

---

**If they say:** "I want to check a few other places first."
**What they mean:** They're price shopping (or quality comparing).

**Your move:** Anchor them to what makes this product worth it.

"For sure. Just so you have something to compare‚Äîthis one has [specific benefit they cared about]. Most people end up coming back to this one because [reason]. But definitely check around!"

**Why this works:** You're planting a seed. When they see cheaper versions that don't have that feature, they'll remember why this one costs more. You sound helpful, not desperate.

---

**If they say:** "I'm just not sure it's what I'm looking for."
**What they mean:** Something's off, but they're being polite.

**Your move:** Ask what's missing.

"What would make it perfect for you?"

**Why this works:** They might tell you: "I wish it came in black" or "I was hoping for something a bit more fitted." Now you can show them alternatives. Or you might discover they need something totally different‚Äîand you can help them find that instead of losing them entirely.

---

### Step 3: Bridge to an Action (Not a Sale)

Once you understand the real objection, don't push for the sale. **Bridge to a smaller action.**

**If price is the concern:**
"Want me to show you one other option that's in a lower range but still really solid?"

**If they need approval:**
"Want me to set this aside for 24 hours while you check with them? No obligation."

**If they're comparing:**
"I'll be here until 7pm if you want to come back after you've looked around. Just ask for me."

**If something's off:**
"Let me show you [alternative]. This one might be closer to what you're picturing."

**Why this works:**
- Low-pressure (not forcing the sale)
- Helpful (solving their problem)
- Keeps the door open (they don't disappear forever)

## üöß COMMON MISTAKES

**Mistake 1: Letting them go too easily**
"Okay, no problem!" is not a strategy. At least try to understand what's holding them back. You're not being pushy‚Äîyou're being helpful.

**Mistake 2: Defending the product**
When they say "I'll think about it," they don't need you to tell them again why it's great. They need you to understand their hesitation.

**Mistake 3: Offering a discount unprompted**
"I can give you 10% off if you buy today!" sounds desperate. Only discount if they explicitly say price is the issue‚Äîand even then, try showing a lower-priced alternative first.

**Mistake 4: Trapping them**
"But you said you needed running shoes! This is perfect for you!" feels like an interrogation. They'll say anything to escape. Give them space, but keep the door open.

**Mistake 5: Taking it personally**
They're not rejecting you. They're making a decision about a product. Stay curious, not defensive.

## üß† WHY THIS WORKS

- They actually wanted something slightly different (you can show them that)

**One luxury retail manager said:**

**The secret:** You're not convincing them. You're uncovering what they need to convince themselves.
